Honoring his college roommate, Gainesville tree farmer dedicates his life to cultivating the canopy

 

“This type of work isn’t to be done in a dress shirt.”

Gene Liddon unclasps the last button of his gator-themed button-down, revealing a plain white T-shirt.

He raises a pair of bright yellow "loppers" to a low branch of a sugar maple. He shimmies the shears around the base of the branch and makes the first cut. 

The first of nine. 

Finally, the branch cracks and falls to the ground. 

"Shoot," he says. "You tell those folks down at UF that I used to be much faster at this." 

Gene Liddon has planted hundreds of trees throughout his life. Probably thousands. He can’t quite remember. But he knows Gainesville, otherwise known as Tree City USA, would look much different without those trees to provide shade and respite from the Florida heat.

“I never kept record of it, but at the university and while I was with the city, we planted a world of trees,” Gene says.

Today, sitting in the center garden of Hunter's Crossing Place, an assisted living facility, his sun-stained hands flip through old photographs as he reflects on his contributions to the canopy.

This is his home now, but for most of his life, his home was in the trees.

Liddon spent nearly 25 years working on Gainesville's treeline, first with the University of Florida, then the city of Gainesville.  

But if not for his college roommate, Gene might have never found his way through the trees.

It was the mid 1940s.

Gene was determined to serve his country and wanted to join the United States Navy. After he and his brother, Sam Liddon, were denied for being too young, Gene attempted to enlist again at 17. This time, his mother joined him to give her permission, but there was a problem.

“The Navy won’t take you if you are colorblind,” Sam said. But Gene persisted.

“Can you cure me of colorblindness?” he asked the physician. An array of flashing colors popped in front of him in a dark room. He wasn’t cured, but he had a memory. The numbers became familiar, and he locked the order of the figures in his mind.

Back in the recruitment office he was given the test. He passed.

In 1945, he said his goodbyes and shipped off to boot camp.

When he arrived, there was a new color vision test. It wasn’t the same. He failed.

“No, you aren’t going to get out that easy and get cold feet now that you are in,” Gene remembers an officer telling him.

He was in.

Gene served two years in the Navy as World War II came to an end. 

In 1948, he came to UF on the G.I. Bill. 

In 1948, campus had a few buildings, barracks on the outskirts to accommodate the influx of students, and only a few trees.

One of Gene’s three roommates, Joey Moody, whom he describes as “a friendly fellow,” was majoring in forestry, and his love for trees and the environment was infectious.

Gene, already a tree climber for the Davey Tree Company, a tree maintenance business, didn’t take much convincing to be sold on a major based on trees.

“If you are jammed in a room, you get to know each other pretty good,” Gene says. 

Joey, always "jolly," had an affinity for trees and spread his knowledge to Gene through conversation and walks through the forest. 

Shortly after Gene changed his major to forestry, Joey made a difficult decision to withdraw from the university. 

Barely two weeks had passed when Gene received word that Joey had been drafted for the Korean War. 

"You were safe from the draft if you were in school," Gene says. "Poor Joey was taken almost immediately." 

One dreary afternoon, Gene heard devastating news.

"He came back in a box," Gene says.

Gene planted trees for years following Joey's death to honor his close friend.  

“[Moody] really got me into forestry and changed my whole life. Forestry moved me to other areas," Gene says.

While studying at UF, Gene started at 90 cents an hour with UF’s grounds department, climbing and pruning trees.

"The work was dangerous," he says. "I climbed trees and I planted trees.

He made his mark near the auditorium and other parts of campus.

Gene moved from working for the grounds department to full-time employment with the city, first as parks superintendent, then as Director of Buildings and Grounds.

Once, the city was clearing trees along a residential road. A woman’s yard was in the path, home to a beautiful live oak set snugly near her house. The tree belonged to the city, but she had made a personal claim. Gene’s crew was at the scene.

Swoosh. The owner of the home swung open the front door, malice in her voice.

"The first one that goes up in that tree," Gene recalls her saying, "I’m going to shoot you out."

“Public Works people were going different ways,” Gene said. “I wanted to plant the trees and they wanted to get rid of everything called a tree out of that side of the road.”

The moment sparked Gene’s fight with the city to save more trees from being cut down.

“Maybe we won’t have to take it down,” he said to the woman.

Gene escorted the city manager to look at the tree, after an extensive dispute. The manager glared at the tree, arms crossed, but gave an approving look.

“He came down on my side,” Gene says. "I go by there once in awhile and it is a great big tree now." 

Trees far enough from the roadway were soon protected by a policy change.

"Gene maintained his enthusiasm for the urban forest,” says Meg Niederhofer, former city arborist. “His wisdom was highly valued by the Florida Urban Forestry Council and I certainly learned much from him."

He’s been a husband for more than 68 years. He is a father to six. He is a grandfather to 17.

In the 1960s, he and his wife, Audrey Liddon, purchased a tree farm and nursery.

"Gene was in the trees then. He is in the trees now. He’s always in the trees."

Today, the walls of the farmhouse are made of cypress. Lights, put up by some of his grandchildren the week before the holiday feast, adorn the trees across the farm. A tire swing sways in the distance, a playhouse sits ready for games, and a turkey slowly cooks. The family, together for their annual Thanksgiving feast, stands around an empty patch of land on the Liddons’ tree farm. They’ve lost a loved one.

“It is our time of memory,” Audrey says.

They watch as Gene takes a shovel to begin digging a hole, with help from his grandchildren.

A tree is planted in remembrance and a plaque is placed.

“My grandfather picks out the tree that we are going to plant and we plant that tree,” says Matt Purdy, the Liddons’ grandson.

The trees he planted throughout the city are mature now.

“I feel satisfied,” Gene says. “Just imagine the city without any trees. It has been a wonderful experience to watch the progression of the trees and a worthwhile endeavor.”

He flips through the last few photos and glances up to catch sight of a pine tree set behind his charcoal-colored roof. He used to be up in those trees. When he wasn't climbing trees, he was planting them. His legacy sprouted over 70 years. 

That legacy is deeply rooted in Gainesville and continues to grow.
